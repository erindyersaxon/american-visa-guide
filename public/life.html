First steps after you land

Your SSN is crucial for employment, banking, and credit. If you selected "Yes" to SSN issuance on your DS-260, Social Security will issue your number automatically within 4–6 weeks of entry.

What to bring to SSA office (if needed):

  • Passport
  • Green card
  • Proof of US address (utility bill, lease, bank statement)

Timeline: Card takes up to 14 days after application. Register for USPS Informed Delivery to track incoming documents.

Open a US bank account immediately for direct deposit and bill payments.

What to bring:

  • Passport or green card
  • SSN (or ITIN initially)
  • Proof of US address (utility bill, lease, bank statement)

Building credit:

UK credit history does not transfer to US credit bureaus. Start with a secured credit card (typically requires $500–$2,500 deposit) to build credit history.

Your state ID is your primary document for identification, driving, and (as of May 2025) domestic flights.

Real ID requirements:

  • Proof of lawful presence: Green card, visa stamp, or EAD
  • SSN documentation: Card, W-2, pay stub, or SSA letter
  • Two residency documents (within 60 days): Utility bill, lease, bank statement, mortgage
  • Matching name: All documents must show the same name

Note: Processing times vary by state (1–4 weeks). Schedule an appointment at your state DMV office.

Green card holders must file annual US tax returns reporting worldwide income regardless of where earned. UK tax obligations do not disappear automatically.

Key forms:

  • FBAR (Foreign Bank Account Reporting): Required if foreign accounts exceed $10,000 combined at any point. File at bsaefiling.fincen.treas.gov. Penalties for non-compliance are severe.
  • FATCA (Form 8938): Required for foreign financial assets exceeding ~$50,000 (accounts, investments, certain pensions).
  • Form 3520: Reports large foreign gifts from family members.

Strongly recommended: Hire an expat tax specialist before your first US filing year (typically April 15 following your entry year).

Males aged 18–25 must register within 30 days of US entry.

Register at: sss.gov/register

Consequences of failing to register:

  • Ineligibility for federal employment
  • Loss of federal student aid and loans
  • Complications with naturalization
  • Potential fines up to $250,000

Global Entry is a CBP trusted traveler program that speeds customs re-entry to the US and includes TSA PreCheck for domestic flights.

Cost & eligibility:

  • Adults: $120 per 5 years
  • Children under 18: Free when enrolled with a parent
  • Payment: Many premium credit cards cover the fee (Chase Sapphire Reserve, American Express Platinum, etc.)

Apply at: cbp.gov/travel/trusted-traveler-programs/global-entry

The $235 immigrant fee is charged by USCIS to produce your green card.

Key points:

  • Timing: Can be paid before you travel (recommended) or after entry.
  • Card processing: Takes up to 90 days from entry date or payment date, whichever is later.
  • IOE number: Your fee receipt includes an IOE (Immigrant Operational Entry) number, distinct from your I-130 IOE. Associated with Form I-551 (permanent resident card).

Pay online at: USCIS Fee Payment Portal

Understanding your status

International travel guidelines:

  • Under 6 months: Green card sufficient for re-entry.
  • 6 months to 1 year: May face questions about abandonment; document US ties (employment letters, tax filings, driver's license).
  • 1+ years: File Form I-131 Reentry Permit before departure (valid up to 2 years, processing 3–6 months).
  • 2+ years: Expired reentry permit requires Returning Resident Visa (SB-1) at a US embassy abroad.

What triggers abandonment concerns:

USCIS evaluates these factors:

  • Duration and frequency of absences
  • Maintained US home
  • Employment in the US
  • US tax filings
  • Family in the US
  • Valid US driver's license
  • Property ownership

Presumption of abandonment: Absences over 6 months are presumed abandonment; over 1 year breaks continuous residence for naturalization purposes.

Filing I-751 is MANDATORY. Not optional. Failure to file before your 2-year conditional card expires will terminate your status.

Timeline:

The 90-day filing window starts 90 days before your card's 2-year expiration. Example: If your card expires June 1, file between March 3–May 31.

Filing options:

  • Joint petition (standard): File with your USC spouse; requires evidence of genuine marriage.
  • Waiver: Available if circumstances changed (divorce, death of spouse, extreme hardship). Requires attorney consultation.

Evidence of genuine marriage:

  • Joint bank account statements
  • Joint lease or mortgage
  • Joint tax returns
  • Insurance policies naming each other as beneficiaries
  • Utility bills in both names
  • Photos of relationship

Citation: INA §216

Filing fee: $790 (includes biometrics). Filing extends your status 48 months while USCIS processes (current processing: 21–24 months).

Eligibility:

  • 3-year rule (if married to US citizen): 3 years continuous LPR residence + 18 months physical presence in the US. Citation: INA §319(a) CR1/IR1 holders must file I-751 before or with N-400.
  • 5-year rule (all others): 5 years continuous LPR residence + 30 months physical presence in the US.

Core requirements:

  • Age 18+
  • Good moral character (no criminal convictions, certain crimes disqualify you)
  • Basic English proficiency (reading, writing, speaking)
  • Pass civics test: 10 questions asked; 6 correct needed

Process:

  1. File N-400 online ($710 fee) with biometrics appointment request
  2. Attend USCIS biometrics appointment (4–6 weeks after filing)
  3. Interview with English/civics exam (typically 6–12 months after filing)
  4. Take Oath of Allegiance at naturalization ceremony (same day or scheduled later)
  5. Receive Certificate of Naturalization

English & civics exemptions:

Free preparation resources:

  • USCIS official materials at uscis.gov/citizenship-test
  • Public library civics classes (available in most US cities)
  • Community college programs (often free or low-cost)
