Master Checklist

Complete, printable checklist for US family immigrant visa consular processing through London — all stages in one scannable reference.

🏦 NVC Stage: Financial Documents

Required financial evidence for your I-864 Affidavit of Support. Submit via CEAC under "Financial Documents."

Primary Sponsor

  • IRS Tax Return Transcript — most recent year, from IRS.gov (create an account via ID.me if you don't have one). Use Tax Return Transcripts specifically — they have been accepted and verified by the IRS. Up to 3 years of transcripts is advised.
  • W-2s / 1099s
  • Proof of current employment — employment letter and recent pay stubs
  • Evidence of US domicile (even if not listed as required) — upload under "Other": driver's licence, job offer, lease, US bills, school enrollment, moving quotation, or intent letter

Joint Sponsor (if applicable)

  • Separate I-864 form
  • Tax Return Transcript (most recent year)
  • W-2s or 1099s
  • Proof of US status and domicile

📄 NVC Stage: Civil Documents

Required civil documents to establish identity, marital status, and criminal record eligibility. All non-English documents require certified translations. Submit via CEAC under "Civil Documents."
  • Birth certificate (long form with parent names)
  • Marriage certificate
  • Divorce or death certificates (if applicable — all prior marriages)
  • UK ACRO Police Certificate — required for all applicants aged 16+. Valid 12 months for embassy purposes regardless of any shorter local validity.
  • Foreign police certificates — required for every country lived in for 12+ months after age 16, or any country of citizenship. Use the decision tree below to confirm what you need.
  • Passport biographic page
  • Court, prison, or military records (if applicable)

Translation note: Check the NVC Document Finder / Reciprocity Schedule for country-specific requirements. Scans must be colour, all pages included, max 4MB per file, PDF preferred.

🏥 Medical Appointment Day

Documents and items to bring to your medical examination appointment at the clinic. Arrive 30–45 minutes early.
  • Passport
  • Interview letter — the email sent to you by NVC notifying you of your interview date and location
  • DS-260 confirmation page
  • Courier registration confirmation (with QR code) — generated when you register your passport return on the AIS website (ais.usvisa-info.com)
  • Completed medical questionnaire (IV18a)
  • ACRO police certificate
  • GP summary dated within 2–3 weeks — does not need to be signed or stamped by the surgery or doctor; a printed or digital summary from your NHS record is sufficient
  • Vaccination records — does not need to be signed or stamped; a printout from your NHS app or GP patient record is accepted
  • Glasses or contact lenses (if applicable)
  • Medical reports for any chronic, serious, or mental health conditions (if applicable)
  • Psychiatric documentation (if applicable)
  • TB or STI records (if applicable)
  • Child health records (if applicable)

🏛️ Interview Day & Document Check

Final documents and items needed for your embassy interview and document verification. The document check typically happens on the same day after your interview.
  • Passport
  • Pink checklist slip (from medical appointment)
  • DS-260 confirmation page
  • Civil documents (originals)
💡 Tip: The document check is usually quick. Staff will confirm everything is in order — many members find this reassuring. Realistic timing: Medical 8:40 AM → finished ~10:15 AM. Document check 11:45 AM → finished ~12:15 PM.

How to use: This template formalises your request for a final decision on your I-130. Send via certified mail to USCIS. Consider having an immigration attorney review or send it on their letterhead — this significantly increases impact. You can also attempt informal resolution (phone call or in-person visit to a USCIS office) before escalating to a formal demand.

[Date]

Sent via Certified Mail – Return Receipt Requested

TO: USCIS Service Center
[Appropriate Service Center Address for your case]
Attn: Case Management Unit

RE: DEMAND FOR FINAL AGENCY ACTION (28 U.S.C. § 1361)
Form I-130 – Petition for Alien Relative
Receipt Number: [NOA1 Number]
Priority Date: [Date]

Dear Sir/Madam,

This letter constitutes a formal demand for final agency action on the above-referenced I-130 petition, filed on [filing date] and assigned priority date [date]. This petition has been unreasonably delayed in violation of applicable law.

BACKGROUND

I, [Petitioner Name], filed the subject I-130 petition on [date] on behalf of my [relationship: spouse/parent/sibling/child] [Beneficiary Name]. The petition was received by USCIS on [date], and I received my Notice of Action (NOA1) confirmation on [date].

As of today's date, [current date], this petition has been pending for [X months/years] — far exceeding normal processing timeframes. The most recent status update available in my USCIS online account is from [date] and shows the case status as "[current status]."

BASIS FOR DEMAND

Under 28 U.S.C. § 1361, applicants have the statutory right to demand that a federal agency take final action on a pending matter when agency action is unreasonably delayed. The Supreme Court in Darby v. Cisneros, 509 U.S. 137 (1993), confirmed that immigration applicants retain this right even with respect to discretionary agency determinations.

USCIS's own published processing times for I-130 petitions are [insert USCIS's published timeframe, typically 12–18 months]. My case has now exceeded these times by [X months], rendering the delay unreasonable and arbitrary.

DEMAND FOR ACTION

I hereby formally demand that USCIS take final action on my I-130 petition by [date 30–60 days from now]. Final action shall mean either:

1. Issuance of a Notice of Approval (NOA2), forwarding my case to the National Visa Center; or
2. Issuance of a formal Notice of Denial with detailed explanation of grounds for denial.

Absent such final action by the above date, I reserve all rights to pursue judicial remedies, including but not limited to filing a writ of mandamus pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 706.

DOCUMENTATION

Attached to this letter are copies of:

• Notice of Action (NOA1) dated [date]
• Most recent USCIS status update (printed from online account)
• Any Request for Evidence (RFE) or other correspondence from USCIS
• Chronology of case events with dates

CONTACT INFORMATION

Please acknowledge receipt of this demand and provide a written response within 15 business days. Contact me at:

Name: [Your Name]
Phone: [Phone Number]
Email: [Email Address]
Mailing Address: [Full Address]

Respectfully,

[Your Signature]
[Your Typed Name]

Submission guidelines
  • Print the letter and sign it by hand.
  • Mail via USPS Certified Mail with Return Receipt Requested — this proves delivery.
  • Send to the USCIS Service Center where your I-130 is being adjudicated (typically listed in your NOA1).
  • Include originals of the attachments (NOA1 and status pages).
  • Keep a copy for your records along with the certified mail receipt.
  • If you have an immigration attorney, consider having them send the letter on their letterhead.

Important: A writ of mandamus is a federal court action. It must be filed by or with the assistance of a qualified immigration attorney. This section provides guidance and key information; it is not a template you can fill in yourself. Work with your attorney to draft the complaint.

⚠️ Prerequisite: Consult an immigration attorney

Before filing, you must consult with an immigration attorney affiliated with AILA. Costs typically range from $1,500–$5,000+. The attorney will review your case, advise on timing and likelihood of success, and either file the action themselves or refer you to litigation counsel.

Key elements of a mandamus action

1. Jurisdiction & Venue
File in the U.S. District Court for the district where you reside or where USCIS has jurisdiction. Naming is typically: "[Your Name] v. U.S. Secretary of Homeland Security" (or USCIS Director). Your attorney will confirm the correct defendant and court.

2. Legal Basis
Cite 5 U.S.C. § 706 (Administrative Procedure Act) and 28 U.S.C. § 1361 (Mandamus statute). The complaint must allege:

  • USCIS has a clear legal duty to adjudicate the I-130 (they do).
  • You have no other adequate remedy (you've exhausted administrative options).
  • Delay is unreasonable (typically 24+ months strengthens the claim).
  • Right to mandamus relief (the agency's inaction is in violation of law).

3. Supporting Documentation
Attach exhibits including:

  • I-130 receipt notice (NOA1) and all USCIS correspondence.
  • Proof of priority date and current case status.
  • USCIS processing time charts showing your case exceeds published timeframes.
  • Timeline of case activity with dates.
  • Evidence of prior attempts to obtain status (emails, USCIS account screenshots).

4. Relief Sought
The complaint will ask the court to order USCIS to:

  • Issue a final decision on the I-130 within a specified timeframe (typically 30–60 days).
  • Pay costs and attorney fees (if successful, you may recover some fees).

5. Timeline & Procedures
Once filed, USCIS has 21–30 days to respond. The judge may grant a preliminary injunction (immediate order) or require further briefing. Cases typically resolve within 3–6 months, though this varies.

Likely outcomes & next steps

Court orders approval of I-130: USCIS appeals or immediately issues approval. Case then proceeds to NVC.

Court orders expedited review: USCIS prioritises the case, typically ruling within 60–90 days.

Case dismissed as premature: Court finds delay not yet unreasonable (less common at 24+ months). You can refile after further delay.

Settlement/Stipulation: USCIS may agree to rule by a specific date, avoiding litigation.

Questions to ask your attorney before filing
  • Does my case meet the legal requirements for mandamus? (Clear duty, no other remedy, unreasonable delay, ripeness.)
  • What is the realistic timeline and cost for litigation?
  • What is the likelihood of success given current case law in my district?
  • Should I try informal resolution (demand letter) first, or proceed directly to court?
  • Will USCIS likely settle or require full litigation?
  • If I lose, am I responsible for USCIS's attorney fees? (Rare but possible.)
  • After we win or settle, what happens — does the case go to NVC automatically, or is there further delay?
