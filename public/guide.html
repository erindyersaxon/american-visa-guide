The Complete London Consular Processing Guide

Everything you need to navigate a US family immigrant visa through the US Embassy London — from filing the I-130 to collecting your passport.

What this guide covers

This guide covers consular processing for family-based immigrant visas via the US Embassy London. It follows the full journey: I-130 at USCIS → National Visa Center → Embassy London interview and visa issuance.

What this guide does NOT cover

  • Local I-130 / Direct Consular Filing (DCF) — a separate process for some applicants already abroad
  • Adjustment of Status (AOS / I-485) — for applicants already in the US. Note: as of May 2026, AOS is now only granted in extraordinary circumstances (see Stage 1 policy update)
  • Non-immigrant (temporary) visas, asylum, or DACA

Core principle

Everything in this guide is based on official US government sources and verified community experience. Where the two differ, the official source takes precedence. Links to official sources are provided throughout.

Before you start

Visa Categories

Family-based immigrant visas fall into two groups: Immediate Relative (IR) visas, which have no annual cap, and Preference (F) visas, which do and can have multi-year waits.

CR1 Spouse of US citizen (married <2 years)

Conditional green card, valid 2 years. Requires filing I-751 (Petition to Remove Conditions) before the card expires. If the marriage crosses 2 years before you enter the US, the visa automatically converts to IR1 — no action needed.

Evidence at filing: joint financial accounts, lease/mortgage, insurance policies, tax returns (joint preferred), photos with name/date/location, travel history and communication logs.

Official: travel.state.gov (opens in new tab)

IR1 Spouse of US citizen (married ≥2 years)

10-year green card. No I-751 required. If you filed as CR1 but your marriage crosses 2 years before US entry, you automatically receive IR1 status on entry — no separate application needed.

Evidence requirements are the same as CR1: joint financial accounts, lease/mortgage, insurance, tax returns, photos with name/date/location, communication logs.

IR2 Unmarried child under 21 of US citizen

Can be CR2/IR2 depending on parents' marriage length. No annual cap.

Evidence: child's birth certificate naming both parents; if stepchild, marriage certificate of petitioner and biological parent; proof of legal custody if petitioner is not the biological parent.

Official: travel.state.gov (opens in new tab)

IR5 Parent of US citizen (petitioner 21+)

No annual cap. 10-year green card.

Evidence: petitioner's birth certificate naming the parent; proof of US citizenship; if sponsoring stepparent, marriage certificate and proof the marriage occurred before the petitioner turned 18.

F1 Unmarried adult child (21+) of US citizen

Subject to annual cap — waits can be several years. Monitor the Visa Bulletin monthly once I-130 is approved.

Evidence: birth certificate; proof of citizenship; evidence of unmarried status; divorce/death cert for any prior marriages.

F2A Spouse and unmarried children under 21 of LPR

Shortest wait of the F preference categories, but still subject to annual cap.

Evidence: marriage/birth certificate; petitioner's green card; prior marriage termination documents; bona fide marriage evidence.

F2B Unmarried adult child (21+) of LPR

Evidence: birth certificate; petitioner's green card; evidence of unmarried status; divorce/death certificates for any prior marriages.

F3 Married child of US citizen

Evidence: birth cert; proof of citizenship; marriage cert; derivative beneficiaries' birth and marriage certificates.

F4 Sibling of US citizen (petitioner 21+)

Waits can exceed 10 years for some countries. File early and be prepared for a long wait.

Evidence: both birth certificates showing a shared parent; proof of citizenship; name change documents if needed; derivatives' birth and marriage certificates.

Stage 1 of 6

USCIS — I-130 Petition

The I-130 establishes a qualifying family relationship. It does not assess finances or issue a visa. The output is an approval notice (NOA2) which forwards the case to the National Visa Center.

⚠️
Policy update — 22 May 2026
Adjustment of Status now limited to extraordinary circumstances

USCIS has announced that Adjustment of Status will be granted only in extraordinary circumstances. Those previously expecting to adjust status from within the United States will now need to follow the consular processing route covered by this guide.

If you have a pending I-485: The memo does not automatically cancel or convert pending applications to consular processing. However, USCIS officers are now directed to deny AOS on discretionary grounds even where statutory eligibility is met, unless extraordinary circumstances apply. Anyone with a pending AOS application should seek qualified immigration legal advice urgently.

Current pipeline status

The front of the I-130 line has not moved since 16 March 2026. As of mid-April 2026, USCIS was only processing cases with priority dates before 25 February 2025. Processing volumes have dropped to less than 50% of pre-slowdown levels. The cause is unconfirmed.

Transgender and non-binary applicants

Under current US policy (April 2025), USCIS determines sex from the original birth certificate and only recognises male or female markers. Inconsistent sex markers can cause delays at USCIS, NVC, and the embassy medical. See the Medical section (Stage 4) for full guidance.

The I-130 Process

  1. 1
    File I-130

    Submit via USCIS Online Account or by mail. Upload civil documents and supporting evidence. Source: uscis.gov/i-130 (opens in new tab)

  2. 2
    Receipt Notice (NOA1)

    Case accepted, priority date assigned. IR visas: typically 12+ months processing, some cases over 2 years. F visas: priority date must be current on the Visa Bulletin before NVC begins — this can take years. Monitor the Visa Bulletin monthly.

  3. 3
    Adjudication

    Status cycles through: Case Was Received → Actively Being Reviewed → approval. You cannot speed this up.

  4. 4
    RFE (if issued)

    USCIS requests missing evidence. The clock pauses until your response is submitted. Front-loading evidence when filing significantly reduces RFE risk.

  5. 5
    Decision (NOA2)

    Approval forwards the case to NVC automatically. Denial allows appeal via I-290B (Motion to Reopen/Reconsider) or refiling. Do not refile without understanding why the case was denied.

Linking family I-130 applications

If filing I-130s for multiple family members simultaneously, USCIS will not automatically link them. You must do this manually.

  1. Label and upload receipt notices to each other's USCIS accounts with label "PLEASE ATTACH – FAMILY CONNECTION"
  2. Cross-upload identity documents (child's birth cert to spouse's I-130 and vice versa)
  3. Complete Form I-130 Part 4, Question 25 listing the other applicant by name and case number
  4. Upload family evidence labelled "FAMILY UNIT EVIDENCE"
  5. Optionally request USCIS to link via the Emma chat tool — optional and may not be acted upon
  6. If one case is stuck at NVC: request expedite on the pending I-130 using "family unity" as the reason
Make it painfully obvious

Clear labels, organised files, and explicit connections matter more than volume. USCIS officers handle thousands of cases — help them help you.

Stage 1B

CRBA — Consular Report of Birth Abroad

If a child is born in the UK to a US citizen parent, they may already be a US citizen at birth and need a CRBA rather than an immigrant visa. This is assessed entirely separately from any I-130.

CRBA vs IR2 — check this first

If the child is a US citizen at birth, filing an I-130 is the wrong route. Assess CRBA eligibility first — it is faster and bypasses the entire visa process.

Jurisdiction

  • Child born in the UK: apply at US Embassy London
  • Child born elsewhere but living in UK: apply in London, but the case is forwarded to the embassy in the country of birth (can take longer)
  • Child already in the US: cannot apply for a CRBA

Physical presence requirement

The US citizen parent must have lived in the US for at least 5 years before the child's birth, including at least 2 years after age 14. Non-continuous periods count. Any time outside the US is excluded, including holidays.

Accepted evidence for physical presence

Accepted

  • Official school transcripts
  • W-2s with HR employer letter
  • Passports showing entries and exits
  • Military records (Statement of Service or DD-214)
  • Bank/credit card statements showing specific US locations
  • US medical records (exact treatment dates only)
  • CBP entry/exit records via FOIA request

NOT accepted

  • US driver's licence
  • Diploma without transcripts
  • Lease or mortgage documents
  • Cell phone records
  • Bank statements without US location shown
  • Social media posts
  • Income tax forms without pay stubs

CRBA application process
  1. Complete eCRBA via MyTravelGov, DS-11 (unsigned), SS-5 (optional, if child is under 5). Upload documents and pay $100 fee online.
  2. Assemble all evidence before booking an appointment. Incomplete evidence is the most common cause of delays and refused appointments.
  3. Pay fees: CRBA $100, passport under 16 $135, passport ages 16–17 $165.
  4. Book appointment: email the London CRBA unit with the child's name, date of birth, and CRBA case number.
  5. Attend interview: child must attend. Both parents should attend; the US citizen parent must attend. If the non-US parent cannot attend, a DS-3053 notarised parental consent form is required (must be within 3 months). Bring originals and photocopies of everything.

Stage 2 of 6

NVC — National Visa Center

After I-130 NOA2, the case transfers automatically to the National Visa Center. NVC does not issue visas — it collects fees and documents, and determines Documentary Qualification (DQ) readiness before sending the case to the embassy.

  1. 1
    Receive NVC Welcome Letter

    You'll receive a Case Number and Invoice ID. Log in at ceac.state.gov/IV (opens in new tab) to begin.

  2. 2
    Pay fees

    IV Application Fee $325, Affidavit of Support fee $120. Both must show PAID before you upload any documents. Uploading before fees clear is a common mistake that delays cases.

  3. 3
    Complete and submit DS-260

    Complete online at CEAC. CEAC times out after ~10 minutes of inactivity and is unreliable — prepare all answers offline first. Once submitted, it cannot be edited. Print the DS-260 confirmation page — you must bring it to the medical and the interview.

  4. 4
    Upload I-864 Affidavit of Support

    Must be wet signed (handwritten signature). Digital and e-signatures are rejected. The standard I-864 is recommended over I-864EZ even for apparently simple cases.

  5. 5
    Upload civil documents

    Always required: birth certificate (long form), marriage certificate, UK ACRO Police Certificate, police certificates from all countries lived in for 6+ months since age 16, passport biographic page.

    If applicable: divorce decrees (all prior marriages), death certificates, court/criminal records, prison records, military records.

  6. 6
    Submit documents

    Uploading alone does not enter you into the review queue. You must click the Submit Documents button separately. This is one of the most common mistakes.

  7. 7
    NVC confirms DQ

    Typically 10 business days (~14 calendar days) after submission. Each RFE resets the 14-day clock from scratch.

I-864 income requirement and joint sponsors

The petitioner must meet 125% of US Federal Poverty Guidelines based on household size. If income is insufficient, irregular, asset-heavy, or earned abroad — a joint sponsor is required.

Joint sponsor requirements

  • Separate I-864 (not I-864A)
  • IRS Tax Transcript (not just a copy of the return)
  • W-2s and/or 1099s
  • Proof of US status (US passport or green card)
  • Proof of US domicile
  • Proof of income: W-2 employees need employment letter + recent pay stubs; self-employed need 12 months of business bank statements; retired need evidence of retirement income
London-specific risk

Asset-only or borderline income cases frequently result in 221(g) delays at London. If there is any doubt about income qualification, secure a joint sponsor before the interview — financial documents cannot be accepted at the window on interview day.

Strategic hold — delaying submission intentionally

Some applicants choose to hold their case at NVC by not clicking Submit Documents, to delay scheduling. Common reasons include needing more time to arrange housing or employment in the US, or waiting for a specific interview window.

This is a legitimate strategy. The case will not move forward until documents are submitted. There is no deadline to submit once fees are paid.

Common failure points at NVC
  • Uploading documents to the wrong CEAC category
  • Missing police certificates for countries lived in for 6+ months
  • Assuming assets suffice without a joint sponsor when income is borderline
  • Submitting before fully ready — every RFE adds ~2 weeks
  • I-864 digitally signed instead of wet signed
  • Police Certificate placeholder not replaced with the actual certificate before interview day
File naming convention

Use: [CASE NUMBER]_[DOCUMENT TYPE]_[NAME]
Example: LNDXXXX_BirthCert_Beneficiary

Stage 3 of 6

Embassy Queue — IL Drops

After achieving DQ at NVC, the case enters the US Embassy London queue. The embassy issues Interview Letters (ILs) in batches — these are called "drops."

How IL drops work

ILs arrive in batches, typically on Tuesday–Thursday between 13:30–15:30 UK time. Each drop covers a range of DQ dates — applicants with older DQ dates are prioritised. Drops are not on a fixed schedule.

Current wait times

  • DQ to IL: the wait has risen from 58 days (all-time average) to approximately 70 days over the last 6 months. Use the 6-month figure as your planning baseline.
  • IL to Interview: typically 8–10 weeks after the IL date.

Stage 4 of 6

Pre-Appointment — Medical & Document Check

Two separate trips to London are required: one for the medical + document check (same day, back to back), and one for the interview (a later date). Do not confuse the two.

Medical must be within 6 months of interview date

The medical is booked at IOM London, not at the embassy. Book it close enough to your interview date that it won't expire, but with enough buffer for any follow-up requirements.

Medical appointment — what to bring

Always required

  • Passport
  • Interview Letter
  • DS-260 confirmation page (printed — not on phone)
  • Completed medical questionnaire IV18a
  • Vaccination records
  • ACRO Police Certificate
  • GP summary (dated within 2–3 weeks)
  • Payment method

If applicable

  • Medical reports for chronic conditions
  • Mental health documentation
  • TB/STI documentation
  • Child health record / Red Book
  • Maternity documentation if pregnant
Medical appointment — what happens
  1. Check-in and digital photo
  2. Document review
  3. Chest X-ray (applicants age 15+)
  4. Blood test (age 18+)
  5. Urine test (age 18+)
  6. Physical examination
  7. Vaccine review and administration of any missing vaccinations
Before you leave the clinic

You must collect your pink checklist slip before leaving IOM. This slip is required for the document check at the embassy the same day. Do not leave without it.

Document check — same day as medical
Go directly to the embassy after the medical

Do not go home first. Go straight from IOM to the embassy. The window closes at 1:00 PM.

Address: 33 Nine Elms Lane, London SW11 7US

Window hours: 11:30 AM–1:00 PM (queue opens ~11:45)

Go to the Pass Back window on the right side of the building — do NOT join the main non-immigrant queue.

Bring

  • Passport
  • Pink medical slip from IOM
  • DS-260 confirmation page
  • Civil documents (originals)

Process

  1. Airport-style security screening
  2. Document check at the window
  3. Fingerprints
  4. Receive sealed "Do Not Tamper" envelope — keep this sealed for interview day

Stage 5 of 6

Interview Day

By this point USCIS, NVC, and the medical have all cleared the case. The interview is a final consistency check. Passport and sealed envelope are the only essentials — the rest is preparation.

Wait is long. Interview is short.

Total time inside the embassy: approximately 90–120 minutes. The interview itself is typically 2–5 minutes. The wait before your window is called is the majority of the time.

What to bring

Essentials

  • Passport (valid, undamaged)
  • Sealed document envelope from document check — do NOT open this
  • Interview Letter
  • DS-260 confirmation page
  • Courier registration confirmation with QR code

Backup documents (rarely requested, but bring originals)

  • ACRO Police Certificate
  • Marriage/birth certificate originals
  • I-864 and any joint sponsor documents
Financial documents must be in CEAC before interview

All financial documents must be uploaded to CEAC before your interview date. Officers will not accept new documents at the window on the day.

Arrival and security
  • Arrive approximately 30 minutes early
  • Go straight to the front — do not join the main non-immigrant visa queue
  • State clearly: "I am here for an immigrant visa interview"
  • Check that your passport sticker has the correct name and case number before leaving security
  • Airport-style security — no laptops, no large bags
  • Bag storage nearby: District Coffee (Nine Elms) or World Heartbeat Cafe
Inside the embassy — two windows
  1. Document window: hand over your passport and sealed envelope, confirm courier registration, fingerprints taken
  2. Interview window: take the oath, answer questions (typically 2–5 minutes), receive the decision — which is usually immediate and verbal
Typical questions by visa category

CR1/IR1 — Spouse of US citizen

  • How and when did you meet?
  • Where does your spouse live? What do they do for work?
  • What do you do for work?
  • Any prior marriages?
  • Have you lived elsewhere or been arrested?
  • Who is your joint sponsor? (if applicable)

IR2 — Child of US citizen

  • Where does your parent live? What do they do for work?
  • When did you last see your parent?
  • Who do you currently live with?
  • Do you have siblings in the US?

IR5 — Parent of US citizen

  • How did your child become a US citizen?
  • Where does your child live? What do they do for work?
  • Who is the joint sponsor? (if applicable)
  • Have you lived in the US before?

General (all categories)

  • Have you ever been arrested?
  • Have you lived in other countries?
  • Have you been married before?
  • What are your plans in the US?
Public charge — voluntary statement

A voluntary public charge statement sent to LNDIVSubmissions@state.gov before the interview removes one potential line of questioning at the window.

Community experience is that it should be sent even though it is described as voluntary. Include your case number in the subject line and send it at least a few days before your appointment.

Post-Approval

After verbal approval at the interview, here's what happens next and what you need to do.

  1. 1
    Passport return

    Returned via courier (registered at booking) or pick-up at Mail Boxes Etc, Vauxhall. Pick-up average: 4 days. Mail average: 12 days. Median overall: ~5 days.

  2. 2
    Pay USCIS Immigrant Fee ($235)

    Pay at my.uscis.gov (opens in new tab). Recommended before travelling to start the green card clock, but can be paid after entry. Do not delay past entry.

  3. 3
    Green card

    Mailed within 90 days of entry to the address on file with USCIS. Confirm your address in your USCIS account after arrival in the US.

  4. 4
    Social Security Number

    SSN initiates automatically if you selected it on the DS-260. If it hasn't arrived within 4–6 weeks of entry, visit your local Social Security Administration office in person.
